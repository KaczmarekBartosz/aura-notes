## Core thesis
- Most pre-profitable solopreneurs are stuck because they:
  - pick weak product ideas,
  - skip strategy clarity,
  - over-rely on one channel (Twitter/Product Hunt),
  - have weak landing pages,
  - quit too early.

---

## 1) Build better products
- Marketing starts with product idea selection.
- Strong idea criteria:
  - simple to build (<1 month for first version),
  - has a major customer insight,
  - clear dominant channel for first 6 months,
  - founder is motivated for long-run.
- "Build a painkiller, not a vitamin."
  - Painkiller: boring, urgent, high consequence if unsolved, frequent usage.
  - Vitamin: hype, low consequence, infrequent usage.

---

## 2) Get specific about strategy
Author suggests answering 7 questions:
1. What one problem am I solving?
2. Which audience segment wants this most?
3. What is the current go-to solution?
4. What is good/bad about that solution?
5. How is my product better?
6. What pricing is a no-brainer and still profitable?
7. How will I get first 10 customers?

Main point: generic answers = weak market understanding.

---

## 3) Go beyond Twitter / Product Hunt
- Launch day virality is rare.
- Product Hunt traffic is short-lived.
- Suggested early channels:
  - social where audience already exists,
  - email list pre-launch,
  - manual cold outreach (especially B2B),
  - Product Hunt as one component, not full strategy.

Post-launch channel ideas:
- free tools for SEO,
- audience building beyond Twitter,
- retargeting ads,
- directory sponsorship,
- newsletter,
- programmatic SEO,
- influencer + affiliate later.

Channels author is skeptical about for solopreneurs:
- newsletter sponsorships,
- referral programs,
- Google Ads early,
- Twitter Ads,
- audience building on Reddit (except long-tail helpful replies).

---

## 4) Fix landing page
Main copy and conversion rules:
- Position around customer outcomes, not product ego.
- Use “you/your” more than “we/our”.
- Show visuals + product video + founder context.
- Add social proof.
- Keep copy simple: emotional promise + rational explanation.
- Keep design constrained (3–4 colors, simple structure).
- Limit long feature lists.
- Remove noisy/weak CTA sections.
- Talk to real users while they navigate landing page.

---

## 5) Try more things
- Nothing works perfectly first try.
- Treat marketing as iterative experimentation.
- If one channel fails, switch, test, and adapt.
- Expect plateaus.
- Consistency + feedback loop wins.

## Practical mini playbooks in the guide

### Marketing copy
- Start with emotion/promise.
- Follow with concrete mechanism.
- Avoid superlatives and buzzword fluff.
- Focus on customer language.

### Getting first feedback
- Find people discussing target keyword.
- Send short manual invites to test product.
- Offer value/free trial for feedback.
- Collect testimonials from best-fit users.

### SEO (author’s simplified model)
- 50% content demand fit,
- 40% links/backlinks,
- 10% technical optimization.
- Favors free tools and intent-driven content over generic blog spam.

### Email marketing
- collect emails with value exchange,
- run lifecycle sequences (lead / signup / customer),
- send regular updates and value.

### Paid ads
- Usually not day-0 move.
- Better after positioning + funnel + conversion are stable.

## FAQ positions in the guide (short)
- Building for indie hackers is fine, but market can be small/price-sensitive.
- Email still works.
- Audience building is optional but useful leverage.
- Weekly focus = choose one objective:
  - improve positioning,
  - increase traffic,
  - increase conversion.
- Good product alone is not enough; distribution matters.

---

## Bias / framing note
The guide is opinionated and intentionally blunt. It is useful as a practical framework, but recommendations should be adapted to product type, audience, and GTM maturity.
